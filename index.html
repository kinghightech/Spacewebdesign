Welcome to the ultimate guide to our solar system

The Sun

The Sun is a hot ball of glowing gases at the heart of our solar system. Its immense gravity holds the entire system together, keeping everything from the largest planets to the smallest debris in a steady orbit. As an ordinary-sized yellow dwarf star, it contains an astonishing 99.8% of all the mass in our solar system. The vast distance between Earth and the Sun is bridged by its light, which travels across 93 million miles of space in just about 8 minutes and 20 seconds to sustain life on our planet.

A Giant Ball of Plasma

The Sun doesn't rotate as a solid object because it is made of plasma. Its equator spins faster, taking about 25 Earth days to complete one rotation, while its poles take up to 35 Earth days to rotate once.

Mind-Boggling Heat

The temperature at the Sun's core reaches a scorching 27 million degrees Fahrenheit (15 million degrees Celsius), driven by nuclear fusion. Even its visible surface (the photosphere) rests at a blistering 10,000 degrees Fahrenheit.

The Solar Wind

The Sun constantly streams charged particles outward into space, creating the solar wind. When these particles collide with Earth's magnetic field, they create dazzling auroras (the Northern and Southern Lights) near our planet's polar regions.

Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and the smallest planet in our solar system, being only slightly larger than Earth's Moon. Named after the swiftest of the ancient Roman gods, it is the fastest planet in our cosmic neighborhood, zipping through space and completing a full orbit around the Sun in just 88 Earth days.

It's Your Birthday Today!

Mercury takes about 59 Earth days to complete one full rotation on its axis. However, because it zips around the Sun so quickly, a single Mercurial year lasts just 88 Earth days.

Extreme Temperature Swings

Because Mercury lacks a substantial atmosphere to trap or distribute heat, temperatures on the sun-facing side scorch up to 450 degrees Celsius, while the side facing away plummets to roughly -180 degrees Celsius.

Ice and Solitude

Deep craters at Mercury's poles never receive sunlight, allowing deposits of water ice to remain permanently frozen in the shadows. Additionally, Mercury and Venus are the only two planets in our solar system that do not have any moons or rings.

Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and Earth's closest planetary neighbor. Often called Earth's twin due to its similar size and density, Venus is a world of fierce extremes. Wrapped in a crushing, toxic atmosphere, it spins backward compared to most other planets, making it one of the most hostile yet fascinating landscapes in the solar system.

The Longest Day

Venus rotates incredibly slowly on its axis, taking about 243 Earth days to complete a single spin. Because it orbits the Sun in 225 Earth days, a single day on Venus lasts longer than its entire year!

Runaway Greenhouse Effect

Even though Mercury is closer to the Sun, Venus is the hottest planet in our solar system. Its thick atmosphere traps heat like a greenhouse, driving surface temperatures to a scorching 475 degrees Celsius—hot enough to melt lead.

Crushing Pressure

Standing on the surface of Venus would feel like being 1 kilometer deep underwater on Earth. The atmospheric pressure is 90 times greater than Earth's, and its skies are filled with heavy clouds of corrosive sulfuric acid.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. Covered mostly by vast oceans, it is uniquely positioned within the solar system's habitable zone. Its atmosphere provides the perfect balance of gases, shielding the surface from harmful radiation while maintaining the stable temperatures necessary to sustain a rich and diverse biosphere.

The Blue Planet

Water covers roughly 71% of Earth's surface, with the remaining 29% consisting of continents and islands. This abundance of liquid water is what gives Earth its vibrant blue appearance from space and makes it entirely unique in our solar system.

A Dynamic Shield

Earth possesses a powerful magnetic field generated by the motion of its liquid iron outer core. This magnetosphere acts as a protective shield, deflecting damaging solar winds and cosmic rays away from the planet.

Not Quite a Perfect Sphere

Because Earth spins on its axis, centrifugal force causes it to bulge slightly at the equator. This shape is known as an oblate spheroid, meaning the distance from Earth's center to the equator is about 21 kilometers greater than to the poles.

Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and the second-smallest planet in our solar system. Often called the "Red Planet" due to the iron oxide on its surface, Mars is a cold, desert world with a thin atmosphere. It is one of the most explored bodies in our solar system, serving as a primary target for the search for signs of ancient microscopic life.

The Largest Volcano

Mars is home to Olympus Mons, the largest volcano in the solar system. This massive shield volcano stands three times taller than Mount Everest and is so wide that its footprint could cover the entire state of Arizona.

A Familiar Day

A Martian day is remarkably similar to an Earth day. Known as a "sol," one rotation on its axis takes 24 hours and 39 minutes. However, because Mars sits further from the Sun, its year lasts almost twice as long at 687 Earth days.

Two Tiny Moons

Unlike Earth's single large moon, Mars has two small, lumpy satellites named Phobos and Deimos. Scientists believe these moons are actually asteroids that were captured by the planet's gravitational pull long ago.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in our solar system—a majestic gas giant more than twice as massive as all the other planets combined. Its immense gravitational pull heavily influences the orbits of surrounding celestial bodies, acting as a cosmic shield that often deflects comets and asteroids away from the inner planets.

The Great Red Spot

Jupiter's most famous feature is the Great Red Spot, a gargantuan, spinning storm that is wider than Earth itself. Astronomers have been observing this raging, high-pressure tempest for over 300 years, though recent data suggests it may slowly be shrinking.

A Mini Solar System

With dozens of moons orbiting it, Jupiter resembles its own miniature solar system. Its four largest satellites—Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto—were discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610 and host fascinating features, including active volcanoes and hidden subsurface oceans.

The Shortest Day

Despite its massive size, Jupiter is the fastest-spinning planet in our solar system. A single rotation on its axis takes just under 10 hours. This rapid rotation flattens the planet slightly at its poles and generates powerful, churning jet streams in its atmosphere.

Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest planet in our solar system. Adorned with a dazzling, complex system of icy rings, this gas giant is a crown jewel of the cosmic neighborhood. Like Jupiter, it is composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, but it holds the unique title of being the least dense planet in our solar system.

Jewelry of the Solar System

Saturn's rings are not solid structures; they are made of billions of individual particles ranging from microscopic dust grains to house-sized boulders. These particles are almost entirely water ice, mixed with rocky debris and cosmic dust, extending up to 282,000 kilometers from the planet.

Lighter Than Water

Despite its massive volume, Saturn is incredibly light because it is made mostly of gases. It has the lowest density of any planet—so low, in fact, that if you could find a bathtub large enough to hold it, Saturn would actually float on water!

Titan: An Earth-Like Moon

Saturn is orbited by a massive flock of moons, the most famous of which is Titan. Titan is larger than the planet Mercury and is the only moon in the solar system with a substantial, thick atmosphere, complete with clouds and liquid methane lakes.

Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and the third-largest planet in our solar system. Classified as an ice giant, it is a pale cyan world distinguished by its unique orientation and extreme atmospheric chemistry. Unlike any other planet in our cosmic neighborhood, Uranus is tilted so far on its side that it essentially rolls around the Sun like a bowling ball.

An Extreme Tilt

Uranus has an axial tilt of nearly 98 degrees. This extreme tilt causes the planet to experience radical seasons; each of its poles spends roughly 21 Earth years bathed in continuous, direct sunlight, followed by 21 years of complete, freezing darkness.

The Ice Giant's Chill

While Neptune is further from the Sun, Uranus actually holds the title for the coldest atmosphere in the solar system. Temperatures here can plummet down to a freezing -224 degrees Celsius, as the planet generates very little internal heat compared to the other gas and ice giants.

Faint Vertical Rings

Uranus possesses a system of 13 known planetary rings. Because the planet is tipped over on its side, these narrow, dark rings appear to orbit vertically around it like a target, rather than horizontally around its equator like Saturn's rings.

Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and most distant major planet from the Sun in our solar system. A dark, cold, and windswept world, this massive ice giant is completely invisible to the naked eye from Earth. It holds the unique distinction of being the first planet in astronomical history to be discovered through mathematical calculations rather than direct visual observation.

Supersonic Winds

Neptune is home to the most severe weather in the solar system. Its atmosphere features whipped-up planetary winds that can reach extraordinary speeds of over 2,000 kilometers per hour—nearly three times faster than the strongest hurricanes ever recorded on Earth.

A Backward Moon

Neptune's largest moon, Triton, is a fascinating and freezing world covered in nitrogen ice. It is the only large moon in our solar system that orbits its planet in the opposite direction of the planet's own rotation, a behavior known as a retrograde orbit.

Deep Ocean Blue

Like Uranus, Neptune's signature blue color comes from the trace amounts of methane gas in its outer atmosphere, which absorbs red light and reflects blue. However, Neptune is a much deeper, more vibrant azure hue, pointing to an unknown atmospheric component that scientists are still studying.
